Overview & Types

What is Phishing?

Phishing is a cyberattack that uses deceptive emails, texts, phone calls, or websites to trick individuals into sharing sensitive information or downloading malware. Unlike attacks targeting systems, phishing exploits human vulnerability through social engineering, manipulating victims into actions that compromise their security.
In a typical phishing scam, hackers pose as trusted entities like colleagues or brands, instructing victims to perform tasks like paying an invoice, opening an attachment, or clicking a link. These actions often lead to stolen personal data, malware installation, or financial fraud, all due to the trust placed in the fake source.

Types of Phishing

Reference

The word "phishing" plays on the fact that scammers use attractive "lures" to trick their victims, much the same way that fishers use bait to hook actual fish. In phishing, the lures are fraudulent messages that appear credible and evoke strong emotions like fear, greed and curiosity. The kinds of lures phishing scammers use depend on whom and what they are after. Some common examples of phishing attacks include:

Bulk email phishing

Bulk email phishing involves sending spam emails to a large number of recipients, hoping some will fall for the scam. These emails often impersonate legitimate businesses like banks or retailers to increase their credibility, using familiar logos, spoofed email addresses, and urgent subject lines like "Problem with your order" to manipulate recipients.
The emails prompt victims to take actions like clicking on malicious links or downloading malware, often leading to stolen login credentials or financial loss. Scammers frequently time their attacks around events like holidays or sales to exploit lowered vigilance, such as during Amazon’s Prime Day.

Spear phishing

Spear phishing is a targeted phishing attack aimed at individuals with access to sensitive data, such as finance managers or executives. Attackers gather personal information about the target from social media or professional networks to craft convincing messages, often posing as someone the target trusts, like a boss or coworker.
For example, a spear phisher might impersonate the target's boss, requesting an urgent task like paying an invoice. When aimed at high-level executives or wealthy individuals, this type of attack is called whale phishing or whaling.

Business email compromise (BEC)

Business Email Compromise (BEC) is a form of spear phishing targeting businesses to steal money or valuable information like trade secrets or customer data. Common BEC tactics include CEO fraud, where scammers impersonate a C-level executive to instruct lower-level employees to transfer funds or send sensitive files, and Email Account Compromise (EAC), where attackers hijack an employee’s account to send fraudulent invoices or request unauthorized payments.
BEC attacks can be extremely costly, with some scams leading to millions in losses. While high-profile scams have targeted large corporations, recent trends show an increase in smaller, more frequent BEC attacks.

Other Phishing techniques

Smishing

Smishing, or SMS phishing, involves scammers sending fake text messages posing as trusted entities, such as wireless providers or shipping companies. These texts may offer "free gifts" or request sensitive information like credit card details, or claim a package fee is required.

Vishing

Vishing, or voice phishing, uses phone calls to trick victims into providing sensitive information. Scammers often spoof caller IDs to appear legitimate and scare recipients with fake issues, such as credit card problems or legal trouble, to extract data or money. Vishing attacks have surged, thanks to VoIP technology.

Social Media Phishing

Social media phishing targets victims through platform messaging features, like Facebook Messenger or LinkedIn InMail. Scammers may pose as users needing help or pretending to offer a contest, aiming to steal login credentials and take over accounts. This is particularly risky for users who reuse passwords across multiple platforms.

How it can be carried out

Execution of Phishing Attack

Here's a breakdown of how phishing attacks are executed, from the initial bait to the eventual exploitation of the victim's information.

Reference

Step 1

The Bait

The attacker sends an email, message, or social media communication that appears to come from a trusted source. Examples include banks, popular websites, or colleagues. These communications often contain urgent calls to action, such as "Your account will be locked," or "You've won a prize."

Step 2

Luring the victim

The message prompts the recipient to click on a malicious link or download an attachment. Often, the link leads to a fake website designed to look identical to the legitimate one. The user is asked to log in or provide sensitive information. The attachment may contain malware compromise victim's device

Step 3

Harvesting Credentials

If the victim enters their credentials or personal information, the data is immediately sent to the attacker. This can include usernames, passwords, credit card numbers, and security questions. The phishing site or malware transfers data to attacker, granting immediate access to victim's accounts.

Step 4

Exploitation

The attacker can then use this information to steal money, compromise other accounts, launch further attacks, or sell the data on the dark web. In the case of spear-phishing (targeted phishing), the attack might compromise business operations, steal intellectual property, or initiate fraudulent transactions.

Signs of Compromise

Identifying Phishing Attacks

Phishing attacks often come with telltale signs that can help you spot them before falling victim. Below are the most common indicators of a phishing attempt—learn to recognize them and protect your sensitive information.

Reference

Unexpected Attachments

Phishing emails often have unexpected attachments, especially with executable files or links.

Urgent Requests

Phishing attacks often create a sense of urgency, such as "Your account will be locked!"

Unfamiliar Sender

Emails from unfamiliar addresses asking for sensitive information are a common phishing tactic.

Suspicious Links

Hover over links before clicking. Phishing links may look legitimate but lead to malicious sites.

Grammatical Errors

Phishing emails often contain poor grammar and spelling mistakes

Mitigation

Security awareness training and organizational policies

Reference

Because phishing scams target people, employees are often an organization's first and last line of defense against these attacks. Organizations can teach users how to recognize the signs of phishing attempts and respond to suspicious emails and text messages. This can include giving employees easy ways to report phishing attempts to the IT or security team. Organizations can also establish policies and practices that make it harder for phishers to succeed. For example, organizations can forbid people from initiating monetary transfers over email. They can require employees to verify requests for money or information by contacting the requester through means other than those provided in the message. For example, employees can type a URL directly into their browser instead of clicking a link or call a colleague's office line instead of replying to a text from an unknown number.

Antiphishing tools and technology

Organizations can bolster employee training and reinforce company policies by implementing advanced security tools to detect phishing attempts. These tools can identify and flag suspicious emails or messages, preventing employees from inadvertently engaging with phishing scams. By doing so, organizations reduce the risk of human error and strengthen their defenses against hackers. Ultimately, these measures help thwart phishing attacks aimed at compromising networks and sensitive data.

Spam filters and email security software

Use data on existing phishing scams and machine learning algorithms to identify phishing emails and other spam messages. The scams and spam are then moved to a separate folder, where malicious links and code are eradicated.

Antivirus and antimalware software

Detect and neutralize malicious files or code in phishing emails by scanning attachments, links, and executables. They use real-time threat detection and behavioral analysis to block malware before it executes. Email filtering features also help prevent phishing emails from reaching the inbox.

Multifactor authentication

Adds extra security by requiring a second form of verification like a fingerprint scan or one-time passcode. While phishers may steal passwords, they struggle to bypass this additional factor, making it harder to take over accounts. MFA reduces the risk of unauthorized access, even if passwords are compromised.

Endpoint security

Tools like Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR) and Unified Endpoint Management (UEM) utilize artificial intelligence (AI) and advanced analytics to detect and block phishing attempts and malware. They continuously monitor devices for suspicious activity and respond to potential threats in real-time, enhancing threat detection accuracy and automating incident response.

Web filters

Prevent users from accessing known malicious websites and provide alerts when they attempt to visit suspicious pages. By blocking harmful content, these tools help reduce the risk of falling victim to phishing attacks and other online threats. Additionally, if a user clicks a phishing link, web filters can mitigate potential damage by preventing further access to harmful sites.

Enterprise cybersecurity solutions

Such as security orchestration, automation and response (SOAR) and security information and event management (SIEM) platforms, use AI and automation to detect and respond to anomalous activity. These solutions can help stop phishers who are attempting to install malware or take over accounts.
